# REFLECTION

## Decisions

One of the biggest choices I made for this project was keeping my setup simple. I stuck with plain HTML, CSS, and just a little JavaScript instead of using any frameworks. Building everything myself helped me understand how the layout worked, how things responded at different screen sizes, and why certain elements behaved the way they did. It also made troubleshooting way easier because I knew exactly what was going on under the hood.

Another major decision was figuring out how to show the family tree visually. In Project 01, I used profile cards, which technically worked but did not really show relationships clearly. As I kept working, I shifted to a vertical tree with connector lines, and eventually reorganized it into a cleaner layout with grandparents, parents, and kids grouped in a way that made more sense. Adding a tree background image helped tie everything together, but I had to tweak it because my first version clashed with the connector lines. Switching to a simpler background fixed that problem and made the design feel more intentional.

I also had a number of ideas for extra features, including more family members, more detailed information, and additional interactivity. I made a conscious decision to stay within the scope of the assignment. Instead of trying to do everything, I focused on getting the core features solid, including navigation, the interactive tree, and responsive layouts. That choice kept the project from becoming messy or unfinished.

Responsiveness was another area where I had to make decisions. I spent time adjusting how the layout behaved on desktop, tablet, and mobile. On smaller screens, I removed the connector lines and background image because they made everything feel cramped. Simplifying the layout on mobile made it much easier to read and use.

## What Worked

The final family tree layout is the part I am happiest with. By the end, the relationships were clear at a glance, especially on desktop where the full structure is visible. Grouping the generations made everything easier to follow.

The background tree image also ended up being a nice addition. Once I found the right kind of image, one without built in nodes or lines, it added personality without getting in the way.

Consistency was another area that worked well. I standardized how names and relationship information were displayed, cleaned up abbreviations, and made sure birth, marriage, and death details followed the same pattern. These small changes made the entire interface feel more polished.

Responsiveness improved significantly over time. I fixed spacing issues on mobile, alignment problems on tablet, and removed visual clutter where needed. Each round of testing helped me identify issues I would not have noticed otherwise.

My workflow using GitHub also worked well. Committing changes regularly, checking the live site, and fixing issues as they appeared helped me stay organized and maintain steady progress.

## What I Would Do Differently

If I were starting over, I would spend more time planning the layout before writing code. I made a number of adjustments early on, especially with spacing and connector lines, that could have been avoided with a clearer plan.

I would also separate my JavaScript into its own file instead of keeping it within the HTML. While the current setup works, separating it would improve organization and make the project easier to maintain.

Another change would be how I approached responsiveness. Instead of fixing issues as they appeared, I would define breakpoints and layout behavior earlier in the process. This would save time and create a more consistent experience across devices.

I also had ideas that I did not implement, such as adding more detailed information for each family member and expanding the tree to include additional generations. If I had more time, I would revisit these ideas and continue developing the project further.

## What I Learned

This project showed me how important the full development process is, not just the final result. Planning, testing, revising, and making small improvements over time all contributed to the final product.

I improved my ability to troubleshoot layout and responsiveness issues. Through repeated testing and adjustments, I became more confident in identifying problems and implementing solutions.

I also learned the importance of managing scope. It is easy to continue adding ideas, but completing a project requires focusing on what is essential. Staying within scope allowed me to deliver a complete and functional final product.

Finally, testing the site across different devices helped me think more like both a developer and a designer. It encouraged me to consider the user experience and make decisions that improved usability and overall design.
